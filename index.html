Project Summary

Diabetes affects millions of Americans, yet most predictive models identify risk factors at the population level, obscuring critical variation across demographic subgroups. This study combines machine learning with systematic subgroup-specific interpretation using the BRFSS dataset to reveal how risk factors differ dramatically across age and income strata.

Key Findings

  • Age-Dependent Risk Patterns: Social determinants dominate diabetes risk among young adults, while BMI emerges as the primary predictor only in older populations (65+).
  • Predictive Success: Transfer learning experiments show models trained on younger cohorts successfully predict future diabetes risk in older groups with 75% accuracy (AUC = 0.749), years before diagnosis.
  • Expanding Health Inequality: The diabetes prevalence gap between income groups widens dramatically (21-fold) from young adulthood through middle age, revealing critical intervention windows.
  • High Model Accuracy: XGBoost achieved the best performance (AUC = 0.811) with SHAP-based interpretability revealing subgroup-specific patterns.

Methodology

Employing XGBoost with SHAP-based interpretability on the 2024 BRFSS dataset (N=133,529), we:

  • Conducted systematic subgroup-specific analysis across age Ã— income strata
  • Applied transfer learning for prospective risk prediction
  • Developed advanced forecasting models with high accuracy (RÂ² = 0.989)
  • Identified critical intervention windows through trajectory analysis

Public Health Implications

These findings challenge one-size-fits-all approaches and provide evidence for population-specific interventions:

  • Young Adults (18-34): Address structural determinants and food insecurity
  • Middle-Aged (35-54): Focus on healthcare engagement and BMI management
  • Seniors (65+): Emphasize chronic disease management

Critical intervention windows identified at ages 25-35 and 40-55 could prevent up to 73% of lifetime health inequality.
